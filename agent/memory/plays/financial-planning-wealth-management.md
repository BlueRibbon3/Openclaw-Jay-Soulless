## 1. Sub-Vertical Identity

Financial planning and wealth management firms help individuals and families manage, grow, and protect their assets. In Australia, operators range from large institutionally-aligned dealer groups (NAB, IOOF, Mercer, VAN) down to independent practices with a handful of advisers. Revenue comes primarily from advice fees — the industry has been transitioning away from commission-based models since the FOFA reforms of 2013. The market comprises approximately 20,000 businesses, with ~26,000 licensed financial planners. The TAM is concentrated: 4–5 major dealer/licensee groups control a significant share of adviser distribution. Most customers of these firms are High Net Worth individuals (HNW — $1m+ in liquid assets), with Funds Under Management (FUM) as the primary business metric.

> The industry is being propped up by an ageing, wealthy population while its structural foundations shift beneath it — and the next generation of clients will not behave like the last.

## 3. Industry Dynamics

**Regulatory restructuring (FOFA)**
The Future of Financial Advice reforms (introduced July 2013) were a direct response to industry scandals — major institutions charging fees for advice never delivered. FOFA mandated professional integrity, transparency, and a client-best-interests duty. The ongoing cost of compliance has been the single biggest structural force reshaping the industry. Smaller firms cannot absorb it; they consolidate or exit.

**Consolidation**
Many smaller independent practices have found FOFA compliance costs prohibitive and have either exited the market or become acquisition targets for larger groups. The result: advisers are migrating away from institutionally-aligned licensees toward non-aligned or self-licensed structures — but even in the non-aligned space, significant churn between practices and licensees continues.

**Fee model transition**
The shift from commission to fee-for-advice has compressed margins and reduced revenue predictability. Profitability pressure is real and ongoing. Clients now explicitly see what they pay, which raises the standard of evidence required to justify advice.

**Demographic tailwind with generational risk**
The ageing Australian population, compulsory superannuation contributions, and rising disposable incomes support sustained demand for financial advice. However: 70% of current clients' children will switch advisers when they inherit wealth. Younger clients want mobile-first, data-driven, automated experiences (Acorns, Stockspot etc.). Firms optimised for the current client cohort are not optimised for the incoming one.

**Trust deficit**
The big four banks and AMP paid $178m in compensation to 200,000 clients for advice fees charged but not delivered. Client trust in institutional advice is structurally lower than in independent practitioners. Separately: in Australia, clients trust family and friends over qualified investment advisers for financial information (Edelman Trust Barometer). This is the operating environment — advisers must earn trust that should be assumed.

**Technology as competitive differentiator**
As price competition intensifies, firms that use technology to reduce the cost of delivering advice and increase personalisation will pull ahead. New tech is focused on managing client accounts, compliance obligations (opt-in renewals, FDS), and administrative functions. The question is not whether to invest in technology — it is which investments compound and which create new overhead.

## 4. Personas

**CEO / Managing Director / Practice Principal**
Cares about: growing FUM, accelerating client acquisition, reducing cost of advice delivery, preparing for the generational wealth transfer, staying ahead of compliance obligations without building a compliance team.
Success looks like: higher revenue per adviser, a referral machine that runs without manual effort, market share growing in the 50+ demographic while beginning to capture the next cohort.
Likely tension: they know their current operating model was built for the last decade, not the next one — but the cost of change feels higher than the cost of standing still.

**Operations / COO / Practice Manager**
Cares about: SOA production time and cost, compliance workflow reliability, reducing the administrative burden on advisers so they can spend more time advising.
Success looks like: cost per SOA down, time to produce SOA down, compliance obligations met without manual chasing, para-planners not bottlenecked.
Likely tension: their workflows are patched together across multiple systems (Xplan, Midwinter, spreadsheets, email) — the integrations are fragile and the data is never quite right.

**Revenue / Business Development**
Cares about: referrals, partner enrolment, conversion from lead to engaged client, cross-sell and upsell across the existing book.
Success looks like: referral process automated, partner channel producing qualified leads, 360° view of the client that surfaces the next-best conversation.
Likely tension: they have the relationship data but it lives in three different places and none of it talks to the others.

**Financial Adviser / Planner (End User)**
Cares about: time per client, quality of advice they can give with confidence, tools that help them do their job rather than create new obligations.
Success looks like: fact find to SOA in fewer touches, client portal that reduces inbound queries, research support that is reliable and current.
Likely tension: they adopted technology to save time and it has added compliance steps instead.

**Their Client (HNW Individual / Family)**
Cares about: being known, personalised advice that reflects their actual situation, responsive access to their adviser, confidence that their assets are being managed with their interests first.
What they expect: 68% are willing to share more personal data than ever before; 77% expect more personalised advice in exchange. Only 18% say their adviser does an excellent job of tailoring advice to them (Accenture Investment Advice Study).
Where the gap is: clients want to feel like the most important relationship their adviser has. Most feel like one of many.

## 5. Jargon

| Term | Definition | Usage note |
|------|-----------|------------|
| AFSL | Australian Financial Services Licence — required to give financial advice. Many ESB customers don't hold their own AFSL; they operate under a dealer group's licence (e.g. Connective). Advisers often mention applying for their own AFSL as a signal of independence intent. | Use when discussing compliance posture and licensee relationships |
| SOA | Statement of Advice — the formal financial plan presented to the client. Complex, compliance-heavy document covering investments, insurance, and tax strategy. Producing it is expensive and time-consuming. | Central to cost-of-delivery conversations |
| FUM | Funds Under Management — the primary business metric. More FUM = more revenue. Losing a client to a competitor or to a robo-adviser is a direct FUM hit. | Use as the north star metric in commercial conversations |
| HNW | High Net Worth — clients with $1m+ in liquid assets. Most advice firm clients are HNW. Complex wealth structures, higher advice complexity, higher fee tolerance. | Use to signal you understand their client base |
| SMSF | Self Managed Super Fund — a common vehicle for HNW clients to manage their own retirement assets. Complex to administer. SMSF growth is a demand driver for advice services. | Use when discussing superannuation and retirement planning context |
| FDS | Fee Disclosure Statement — required under FOFA. Annual document showing what was charged and what services were provided. Non-compliance is a regulatory risk. | Use in compliance burden conversations |
| FoFA | Future of Financial Advice — the reform legislation introduced 2013. Reshaped the entire industry. Everything about the compliance burden traces back here. | Frame as the forcing function for all structural change |
| Dealer Group / Licensee | The entity that holds the AFSL and under which individual advisers practice. Major groups: NAB, IOOF, Mercer, VAN. Compliance happens at the dealer group level. | Critical for understanding buying dynamics — the dealer group is often the real decision maker |
| Para-planner | Support role that assists advisers with research and SOA preparation. Bottleneck point in advice delivery. Change management risk when introducing new technology. | Use when discussing workflow and cost of delivery |
| Fact Find | The initial client discovery process — gathering goals, assets, liabilities, insurance, tax position. Also called the 'application process'. Starting point of the client lifecycle. | Use to show familiarity with how advice actually begins |

## 6. Client Lifecycle

**Stage 1: Lead / Referral**
How clients arrive: primarily through referrals from existing clients, partner networks, and dealer group introductions. Referral quality varies significantly. Most practices do not have a systematic referral process — it depends on individual adviser relationships. Lead management is the first place technology can create leverage.

**Stage 2: Identify Goals (Fact Find)**
The adviser works with the prospective client to understand their short and long-term financial goals, assets, liabilities, insurance, and tax situation. This is known as the 'fact find' or 'application process'. It is the foundation for everything downstream. Where it is done poorly, the SOA suffers. Where it is done well, the client feels known.

**Stage 3: Assess Financial Situation**
The adviser analyses the client's full financial position. Third-party data and tools come in here (Xplan, Midwinter, Coin). This stage is research-heavy and time-consuming for para-planners.

**Stage 4: Prepare the Financial Plan (SOA)**
The most expensive stage. Strategies, products, and services are identified and documented in the Statement of Advice. Compliance requirements mean this document is complex and costly to produce. Cost and time per SOA is the key operational metric here.

**Stage 5: Present the Plan**
Adviser meets with the client to present the plan. Covers investments, insurance, trusts, tax. The client portal and community tools are relevant here — some firms are moving to digital plan presentation.

**Stage 6: Implement the Recommendations**
The plan is put into action. Third-party specialists (accountants, solicitors) may be involved. This is where integration between systems matters most — handoffs between the advice platform and execution tools create friction.

**Stage 7: Ongoing Reviews**
Client circumstances, lifestyle, and financial goals change. Regular reviews are both a compliance obligation and the primary retention and upsell mechanism. This stage runs indefinitely and is where the referral loop restarts. Marketing Cloud, Community, and automated review triggers are relevant here.

## 7. SWOT Snapshot

**Strengths**
- Demographic tailwind: ageing population, compulsory superannuation, and rising disposable incomes structurally support demand regardless of operational quality
- High-income earner concentration: HNW clients have complex needs, high fee tolerance, and low price sensitivity relative to the value of advice
- Referral economics: loyal clients generate referrals — practices with strong relationships grow without proportional marketing spend
- Stock market correlation: rising index performance increases investor confidence and drives consumption of advice services

**Weaknesses**
- Low revenue growth: commission-to-fee transition and reduced consumer confidence have compressed margins; revenue growing at 0.7–0.8% annually — slower than the broader economy
- Profitability pressure: compliance costs, fee model changes, and adviser churn have hit independent practices hardest
- Trust deficit: industry scandals have reduced the default credibility advisers once enjoyed; every adviser now carries the weight of institutional misconduct they had no part in
- Fragmented technology stack: most practices run across multiple disconnected tools — Xplan, Midwinter, Coin, spreadsheets — creating data gaps and manual overhead

**Opportunities**
- Disruptive technology: firms that use technology to reduce cost of delivery and increase personalisation gain a structural cost advantage and a client experience advantage simultaneously
- FOFA compliance as differentiator: firms already compliant can compete more effectively while others are distracted by catching up
- Increased demand: superannuation guarantee growth, SMSF expansion, and ageing population will sustain and grow demand for advice services over the next decade
- Generational wealth transfer: $3.5 trillion in assets will transfer to the next generation over the next 20 years — firms that build a relationship with clients' children now have a structural advantage

**Threats**
- Increased competition from adjacent industries: CPA Australia was granted an AFSL; accountants can now provide SMSF-related services and are expected to gradually expand their advice footprint
- Robo-advisers and fintechs: Acorns, Stockspot, and similar platforms are capturing the next generation of clients with mobile-first, low-cost alternatives
- Reducing fees: price competition is intensifying; profitability will continue to fall as firms lower prices to retain clients
- Higher compliance and training costs: FOFA obligations continue to evolve; the cost of staying compliant rises faster than revenue for smaller operators

## 8. Competitive Landscape

**Direct competitors**
- MS Dynamics: primary CRM competitor. Strengths: enterprise credibility, Microsoft ecosystem integration. Weakness: not purpose-built for financial advice workflows; compliance capability requires significant customisation.
- Practifi (AppExchange Partner): built on Salesforce platform. Financial advice-specific workflows. Relevant because it sits on the same platform — the conversation is about build vs. buy vs. configure.

**Incumbent planning tools for integration** *(these stay — integration is the conversation)*
- Xplan: dominant financial planning software in Australia. Advisers are deeply embedded. Not being replaced. Integration quality determines whether the CRM is seen as additive or duplicative.
- Midwinter / Rubic (COIN): planning and compliance tools. Same dynamic as Xplan — integration matters more than replacement.

**Alternative blueprints** *(point solutions and adjacent approaches)*
- Midwinter, Advisor Logic, Class Super, Ignition Wealth: practice management and planning tools that compete for parts of the stack. Each solves a specific problem well but none provide the full client engagement platform.

**Common objections rooted in this landscape**
- "It's too expensive": Fee pressure is real. This is often a proxy for "I don't see where the ROI is." The conversation needs to move to cost per SOA, referral volume, and adviser capacity before price is relevant.
- "We already use Xplan / Midwinter": Integration story is the answer. The question is not whether to replace their planning tool — it is whether their client engagement layer is as strong as their planning layer.
- "Advisers don't want to own another system": Change management and para-planner resistance are real. Adoption requires showing the adviser what changes in their day, not what new features they get.
- "Compliance happens at the dealer group level": True — and the dealer group is often the real decision-maker. Engaging the licensee is sometimes the more important conversation than engaging the practice.

## 9. Key Success Factors

**Ability to communicate and negotiate**
Financial planners and advisers must communicate complex strategies clearly to clients and negotiate fee structures with product suppliers. Firms that systemise this — templated client communications, automated follow-up, consistent messaging — pull ahead of those who rely on individual adviser skill.

**Maintaining a loyal client base through referrals**
Growth in this industry is referral-driven. A satisfied HNW client who refers one family member per year is worth multiples of their own FUM. Firms that track, measure, and systematise referrals compound faster than those that rely on organic word-of-mouth.

**Regulatory compliance as a baseline, not a ceiling**
Must-have AFSL licence compliance is the floor. Firms that treat compliance as infrastructure — automated FDS, audit trails, opt-in renewal workflows — free adviser time for what matters. Firms that treat it as overhead are perpetually distracted by it.

**Research quality and reliability**
Sound advice depends on reliable market intelligence and product knowledge. The quality of third-party research support and the reliability of integration between planning tools and market data is a direct input to advice quality and adviser confidence.

## 11. Signal Indicators

**Strong signals — recommend this pack**
- Job posting for a Head of Operations, Chief of Staff, or Practice Manager mentioning "compliance" or "SOA" — signals operational investment and likely pain around advice delivery cost
- Executive commentary referencing "client experience," "digital advice," or "next-generation clients" — signals awareness of the generational challenge
- Recent acquisition of another practice or merger with a dealer group — consolidation in progress; systems are in flux
- News coverage or LinkedIn activity referencing ASIC, AFSL review, or compliance audit — regulatory pressure is live
- Tech stack showing Xplan or Midwinter with no visible CRM — the planning layer exists without a client engagement layer
- Financial report showing flat or declining revenue despite FUM growth — margin compression is the pain

**Weak signals — consider alongside other packs**
- Generic "digital transformation" language without advice-specific context — may be broad IT initiative, not advice-specific
- Small practice (<5 advisers) — buying dynamics shift toward individual adviser preference; dealer group conversation may not apply

- LinkedIn — adviser and executive movement signals, job postings

## 12. Do-Not-Use Conditions

- **Prospect is a retail bank or insurance company**: The compliance and advice delivery framing does not apply. Use a broader FSI pack instead.
- **Prospect is a superannuation fund (not-for-profit)**: Member-not-customer language and fund governance dynamics require a separate frame.
- **Prospect is a robo-adviser or fintech**: They are the disruptor in this frame, not the disrupted. The tension does not land.
- **Primary pain is clearly in a different domain**: If discovery has surfaced that the core problem is IT infrastructure, HR, or supply chain, this pack adds noise rather than credibility.
- **Net-new account with no financial services context surfaced**: Do not use sub-vertical framing speculatively. Wait for at least one strong signal before loading.
