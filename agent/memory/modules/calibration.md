## Calibration 1 — Not every dollar is equal

**The principle.**
A $40,000 opportunity at a large established enterprise and a $40,000 new business opportunity at a high-growth fintech are not the same thing. Segment, trajectory, access quality, and long-term account value are all different. Measuring deals only by immediate contract value systematically underweights the accounts that will matter most in three to five years.

Big deals are won when all stakeholders agree on what is at stake, what problem is being solved, and what it is costing the business — in lost productivity, lost revenue, or spend on things that are not working. The transaction size follows from the problem size. The entry point is always the problem, never the number.

**The situation.**
Running the Fintech team at Salesforce Australia. Market moving fast — fintechs emerging locally following US patterns. Deliberate play: find businesses before competitors, before they scaled, before procurement layers existed, before another vendor was embedded.

Prosper (P-r-o-s-p-a) — small business lender, ~15 employees including founders, filling a gap the major banks were not playing in. Outcome: 15 Unlimited Edition CRM licenses, wall to wall. Significant investment for a 15-person business. Meaningful deal by segment metrics.

Competitive alternative was NetSuite — not a feature comparison but a trajectory question. Had the decision gone that way, the following decade looked entirely different for both sides.

Ten years later: still a customer, expanded across almost every product area.

**Agent instruction.**
When evaluating a prospect, do not anchor on immediate deal size as the primary measure of opportunity quality. Weight the following independently:

- *Trajectory* — is this business growing, and at what rate? A small business with strong growth signals is a different investment than a static one.
- *Access* — how direct is the line to senior decision-makers? Founders at a 15-person company are accessible in a way they are not at 150. That window closes and does not reopen.
- *Competitive displacement risk* — if a competitor embeds now, how hard is it to displace them later? Early-stage businesses that commit to a platform tend to stay on it.
- *Compounding value* — what does this account look like if the business achieves its growth goals? Model the three-year scenario, not just the current opportunity.

When the Researcher surfaces growth signals — hiring pace, funding rounds, market expansion, founder-level press activity — flag them explicitly in the prospect brief and weight the opportunity accordingly. A deal that looks modest today may be the most important account in the portfolio in three years.
