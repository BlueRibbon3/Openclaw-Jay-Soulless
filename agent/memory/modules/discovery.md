## Pre-call prep sequence

1. **Intro email** — sent before the first call. Agenda, immediate value (relevant customer story or industry insight). Not a calendar confirmation — it is the first signal of how this engagement will run. Customer stories: retrieve via `site:salesforce.com customer stories [industry]` — use entry point, not hardcoded deep links.
2. **Discovery call** — entirely in the customer's language. Never lead with product or service names. Goal: current state, individual and business priorities, issues and goals, KPIs, challenges, strategic initiatives. Get to numbers. Identify who else can complete the picture.
3. **Get to power and stay there.** Business holds the biggest budgets and is closest to the problems. IT supports the business. Operations supports sales and service. Find the business owner, not the technical gatekeeper.
4. **Playback** — close the discovery call by reflecting back what was learned with examples of customers who faced similar challenges. Does not need to be a public case study.
5. **Outline the evaluation process** — do not assume the customer knows how to evaluate enterprise technology. Guide them with an evaluation plan presented as a starting point they can adjust.
6. **Close with next steps** — defined action, owner, date. Not "I'll follow up."
7. **Post-call recap email** — sent immediately. Recaps discussion and agreed items. Provides next step date and time. Documents open questions for both parties. Ask for "ok" reply or inline edits.
8. **Partner or SE introduction** — at this stage only, where appropriate. Not a default add-on.
9. **Further discovery with other departments** — for complex or large accounts.
10. **Demo — held as late as possible.** The more that is understood before the demo, the more tailored it will be. Holding it late maintains control and filters out tyre kickers.
11. **ROI** — particularly important against lower-priced incumbents. Identify what will make a meaningful ROI case during discovery and gather the data throughout — do not wait until the end.

**Power map hypothesis (before first meeting):**
- Who holds the budget? (Almost always the business, not IT)
- Who has the problem? (Often not the same person)
- Who is the likely champion? (Someone who benefits and has internal credibility)
- Who are the likely blockers? (Procurement, IT gatekeepers, incumbent vendors, politics)

## Discovery posture

**Discovery is always on.** Not a phase — a permanent mode. Finding out late from a blocker is worse than finding out early from a conversation. Surface and frame new issues on your terms, not someone else's.

**Go in with a broad hypothesis.** High growth → hiring and scaling problems. Contraction → efficiency and do-more-with-less. Sharpen based on industry and size, but keep it wide enough that a few data points in the room can confirm, pivot, or narrow it fast.

**The upfront contract — word for word:**
*"Hello, thank you for taking the time to meet with me. My name is Jay and I am responsible for [territory/accounts]. I have been doing this for X years and have worked with Y organisations in this capacity. Over the next 30 minutes I will be asking you questions to better understand your business goals and challenges, with an aim to provide you with a tailored point of view on how Salesforce can help you. This is not meant to be an interrogation — if you have questions for me, please ask them. At the end of this call, my commitment to you is this: if I believe there is value in continuing these discussions, I will tell you. If I believe we are not in a strong position to assist, I will let you know and we can go our separate ways. I ask the same of you — if at any point you don't see value in continuing, please tell me. After this meeting I will summarise our discussion and provide you with example customer stories, use cases and a high-level view of potential value. Does that sound ok?"*

**Open with industry-level pain, not a direct accusation:**
*"Many organisations I speak with are struggling with [X]. How does [company] approach that?"* — observational, not accusatory. Invites self-identification.

**Never ask yes/no questions.** Every question generates more information than existed before asking it.

**L1 → L2 → L3 pain funnel:**
- L1: Operational pain (what is the symptom?)
- L2: Business impact (what is it costing — revenue, productivity, time?)
- L3: Personal/emotional cost (what does this mean for the person in the room?)

Stay in L3 once you find it. That is where the real decision-making lives.

## Question toolkit

**The Sequence** — *"Walk me through a day/week/month in your role — what does that look like?"* Entry door varies by function: Sales entry, Service entry, Finance entry, Operations entry. Follow the thread once it surfaces.

**L1/L2/L3 Pain Funnel** — *"When that happens, what's the impact on the business? And for you personally — what does that mean?"* Never stop at L1. The business case lives at L2. The decision lives at L3.

**Other Viewpoint Comparison** — *"What would your CEO think about that? What would the people on your frontline say?"* Steps them out of their own perspective. Ambition, insecurity, and political positioning become visible here.

**Naive Outsider Perspective** — *"I don't know much about how this works in your organisation — can you walk me through it as if I'd never seen it before?"* Removes assumptions. They stop abbreviating; you stop filling gaps with guesswork.

**Quantity** — *"How many times a week does your team deal with X? How many people are affected?"* Discovery without numbers is opinion. Even rough numbers begin the business case.

**Changes Over Time** — *"How is this different from twelve months ago? Three years ago?"* Direction and velocity matter as much as current state. A problem getting worse is more urgent than one that is stable.

**Tasks and Structure** — *"Can you describe how information moves through your team on a typical day?"* When someone maps their own process, gaps and dependencies that are normally glossed over become visible.

**Suggestive Opinion** — *"I speak with a lot of organisations in your space. Some have strong views on X. Where do you land?"* Tests a hypothesis without stating it as a conclusion. Use after rapport is established — not before.

**Process and commitment questions** (used at transition points when a theme has emerged):
- *"When we finalise the business case, who else needs to review it?"*
- *"If everything makes sense and the value is clear — what would need to be true for your organisation to move forward?"*
- *"Who else would need to be part of this conversation before a decision could be made?"*
- *"Has your organisation made a similar investment before? How did that process work?"*

**Agent rules — question toolkit:**
- The Sequence question is almost always the right entry point.
- X is not pre-filled — it emerges from what the prospect says.
- Never ask yes/no questions.
- When quantitative data surfaces, pursue it.
- Suggestive Opinion and Other Viewpoint Comparison carry the most political risk — use after rapport.
- The goal of the entire toolkit is L3 pain. All other question types are the path to that destination.

## The Mutual Success Plan (MSP)

**What it is:** A shared document — owned by the AE, co-created with the customer, visible to the entire account team. Generated after first substantive interaction. Evolves throughout the cycle. Not a sales control tool — a service to the customer.

**One job:** Make it easy for the customer to buy. Reduce decisions to what is necessary to reach yes or no. Get to yes or no fast. Treat a slow maybe as the outcome to avoid above all others.

**Introduced at end of first discovery call:**
*"Thank you for your time. I will sync with my team and come back with a proposed path forward. The document that helps us stay on track is called a Mutual Success Plan. I will have this to you within 24 hours. Please read through and share any feedback. This is a living document — the framework provides structure and keeps us on track."*

The 24-hour turnaround is a signal, not a courtesy. It demonstrates that what was discussed was heard.

**MSP must be readable in five minutes.** If it takes longer, it has drifted from its purpose.

**Contents:**
- *Opportunity Statement* — written from the customer's lens (shared aspiration + honest current state). Never the vendor's diagnostic lens.
- *Key Stakeholders* — everyone involved on both sides (vendor, customer, partner) with their role in this specific project.
- *Timeline* — activities completed and remaining. Only high-value activities. Goal: get to the stakeholders who matter and stay there.
- *Next Actions* — specific, with clear ownership: Salesforce, Customer, or Partner. No vague actions.

**Close-out ritual (every meeting):**
*"Before we wrap up — let me play back what we have covered, what we have agreed, and what happens next. Tell me if I have missed anything."* Deliver playback. Wait. Let them respond. What is agreed goes into the MSP.

**After every meeting:** structured summary generated (from transcript or notes), plain text, formatted for copy-paste. Account team reviews and stress-tests the opportunity statement, POV, and value drivers.

**Agent rules — MSP:**
- MSP is generated by the AE. Researcher and BDR inform it but do not generate it.
- Opportunity Statement is always written from the customer's lens.
- Never put technical requirements, internal open questions, or budget/sign-off detail in the MSP.
- Readable in five minutes. If longer, it has drifted.
- After every meeting: playback, update MSP, record next actions with owners, calendar invite, structured summary for account team.
