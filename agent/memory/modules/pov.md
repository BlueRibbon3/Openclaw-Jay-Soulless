## What a POV is

A POV is not a pitch. Not a product brochure with the customer's logo on it. It is a perspective — developed from research and discovery — that shows the customer something true about their situation that they have not fully seen yet.

Its job in the early cycle: earn the right to a conversation. Show that you have done the work. Demonstrate curiosity, respect, and a genuine desire to be a partner. Trust is the currency. The POV is how you make the first deposit.

Later in the cycle, when you are in a competitive evaluation and the cost of inaction needs to be made explicit, the tone can sharpen. But directness is only earned after trust has been established. Lead with partnership. Follow with challenge. Never reverse the order.

---

## Structure of an early POV

1. **Start with their world, not yours.** Open with the customer's customer, or the customer's frontline — whoever is closest to the problem. This signals that you have thought beyond the org chart.

2. **Validate what they are already doing.** Acknowledge the investments they have made and the thinking behind them. Do not skip this. Executives who feel their existing decisions are being dismissed stop listening.

3. **Introduce the gap as an opportunity, not a failure.** The transition from what they are doing well to what is possible should feel like an invitation, not an indictment. "To unlock greater customer experience... the team needs to be armed with just-in-time insights." Not: "Your current approach is reactive and leaving value on the table."

4. **Make it concrete and specific.** Abstract POVs get ignored. Specificity signals real research. Generic observations that could apply to any company in the sector are a failure of preparation.

5. **End with aspiration, not an ask.** Close on what becomes possible. The ask is implicit — if this resonates, let us talk. You do not need to say it.

---

## What a POV must never do

- Name a product before the problem is established
- Tell the customer what is wrong with their business
- Use generic language that could apply to any company
- Include content about your company's history, headcount, or awards
- End with a feature list
- Open with the vendor's perspective

---

## The provocative question

Every POV is built backwards from a single question — the question you want the executive sitting with after they have read it. Decided first. The entire POV earns the right to ask it. Specific enough to feel personal, open enough to invite a real answer, uncomfortable enough to signal you have thought seriously about their situation.

Decide the question. Then build backwards from it.

---

## Language discipline

Use the customer's own vocabulary throughout. Extract it from their website, annual reports, press releases, and job postings before drafting. Never introduce a product name before establishing a shared problem definition. If a product name must be referenced, frame it as a capability or outcome first.

The CBUS principle: a single correct word — *members* instead of *customers* — can establish credibility that no slide deck can manufacture. Get the language right before you get into the room.

## POV generation rules for JaysAgent

- Decide the provocative question first. Build backwards from it.
- Open with the customer's world. Never open with the vendor.
- Use the customer's language throughout. Extract vocabulary from their public materials before drafting.
- Validate existing investments before surfacing gaps.
- Frame gaps as opportunities, never failures.
- End with aspiration. The ask is implicit.
- Never name a product in an early POV. Describe the capability or outcome instead.
- Run red team analysis after drafting: what is the strongest counter-argument a well-prepared customer could make? What is missing? What would make an executive dismiss it?

## POV critique rules for JaysAgent

When critiquing a draft POV, assess against these dimensions in order:
1. Does it open with the customer's world or the vendor's?
2. Is the provocative question identifiable — and is it specific enough to feel personal?
3. Does it validate existing investments before surfacing gaps?
4. Are gaps framed as opportunities or as failures/indictments?
5. Does it use the customer's own language or generic industry language?
6. Does it name a product before establishing the problem?
7. Does it end with aspiration or with a feature list / explicit ask?
8. What is the strongest counter-argument against the central hypothesis?
9. What is missing that a well-prepared customer would immediately notice?
